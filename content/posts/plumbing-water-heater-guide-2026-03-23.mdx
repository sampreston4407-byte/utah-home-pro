# Utah Homeowner's Guide to Water Heaters: When to Repair, When to Replace

Your water heater is one of those appliances you never think about until it fails — usually on a cold morning, usually at the worst possible time. In Utah's climate, understanding the basics can help you plan ahead instead of react in a panic.

## How Long Do Water Heaters Last in Utah?

The standard tank water heater lasts 8–12 years. Tankless units last considerably longer — 15–20 years with proper maintenance. Utah's hard water is a real factor here: the high mineral content along the Wasatch Front accelerates sediment buildup inside tanks, which reduces efficiency and shortens lifespan. If you have hard water and aren't flushing your tank annually, you're shortening its life significantly.

## Warning Signs It's Time to Act

Don't wait for a flood. These are the signs a water heater is nearing the end:

- **Rusty or discolored water** coming from hot taps — the tank is corroding from inside
- **Rumbling or popping sounds** — sediment has built up and is boiling inside the tank
- **Water pooling around the base** — a sign the tank wall has developed a slow leak
- **Inconsistent hot water** — taking longer to heat up, or running out faster than it used to
- **Age over 10 years** — even if it appears to be working, internal corrosion may be advanced

A slow leak left unchecked can turn into a full tank failure, which means 40–50 gallons of water in your utility room or garage.

## Tank vs. Tankless: What Makes Sense in Utah?

Both options work well here, but there are tradeoffs worth knowing:

**Tank water heaters** cost less upfront ($600–$1,200 installed) and are simpler to repair. The downside is standby heat loss — you're paying to keep 40–50 gallons hot around the clock whether you use it or not.

**Tankless water heaters** cost more upfront ($1,500–$3,000 installed) but only heat water on demand. They're more energy-efficient over time and eliminate the risk of a tank bursting. The catch: they require adequate gas line pressure and proper venting, and Utah's hard water means the heat exchanger needs descaling every 1–2 years.

For larger households in Utah County or Salt Lake County that use a lot of hot water, tankless often pays back the premium within 5–7 years. For smaller households or rentals, a quality tank unit is usually the practical choice.

## What a Water Heater Replacement Actually Costs

In most Utah markets, a straightforward tank replacement runs $900–$1,600 all-in, including labor. Tankless installs range from $1,800–$3,500 depending on whether gas line or venting work is needed.

Factors that drive up the cost:
- Upgrading from tank to tankless (new venting and gas line work)
- Moving the unit to a different location
- Bringing older installations up to current Utah code

## Maintenance That Extends Life

One simple habit extends tank life significantly: **flushing sediment once a year**. Attach a hose to the drain valve, run water until it clears, and you'll prevent the buildup that causes most early failures. Also test the pressure relief valve annually — it's a safety device, not just a component.

Hard water areas benefit from a whole-home water softener, which protects not just the water heater but all your appliances and fixtures. It's worth factoring into any longer-term plumbing decision.
